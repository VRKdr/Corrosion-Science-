Corrosion and Its Control

What is Corrosion?

Corrosion is an irreversible process in which metals, except noble metals, are converted into stable compounds like oxides, sulphides, hydroxides, and carbonates. The most common example is the rusting of iron (Fe2O3.xH2O).

Electrochemical Theory of Corrosion

When metals are exposed to moisture, they undergo corrosion through electrochemical processes, forming separate anode and cathode areas.

  • Corrosion occurs at the anode through oxidation.
  • Electrons move towards the cathode where reduction occurs.
  • Types of electrochemical corrosion: Hydrogen Liberation and Oxygen Absorption.

Types of Corrosion

  • Galvanic Corrosion: Occurs when two dissimilar metals are electrically connected in an electrolyte.
  • Corrosion due to Differential Aeration: Caused by different oxygen concentrations in metal exposure.
  • Uniform Corrosion: A general attack where the entire surface corrodes uniformly.
  • Pitting Corrosion: Localized corrosion leading to cavity formation.
  • Stress Corrosion: Combined effect of tensile stress and corrosive environment.

Cathodic Protection

Cathodic protection prevents corrosion by making the entire metal surface act as a cathode.

  • Sacrificial Anodic Protection: Using a more active metal (like Zn, Mg) to corrode instead.
  • Impressed Current Protection: Applying a direct current to prevent corrosion.

Protective Coatings - Paints

Paints act as a protective barrier against corrosion. They consist of:

  • Pigments: Provide color and protection.
  • Vehicle (Drying Oil): Forms a protective film.
  • Thinners: Reduce viscosity for easy application.
  • Driers: Accelerate drying.
  • Fillers: Improve properties and reduce costs.
  • Plasticizers: Provide elasticity and prevent cracking.
